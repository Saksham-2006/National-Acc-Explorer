## Executive Summary

Over the 2015–2025 decade, the national economy grew by **64.5%** in nominal GDP terms, rising from $18.2T to $29.95T. The period was defined by three distinct phases: a steady pre-pandemic expansion (2015–2019), a sharp pandemic contraction and recovery (2020–2021), and a post-stimulus normalization with persistent but declining inflation (2022–2025). As of 2025, the economy is in a healthy equilibrium — GDP growth at 2.5%, inflation returning to target at 2.6%, and unemployment near historic lows at 3.9%. However, elevated Net Domestic Product depreciation levels and the services sector's growing dominance warrant close monitoring.

### Critical Analysis

**The Deflationary Risk of 2015 (0.12%):** Near-zero inflation in 2015 indicated extremely weak consumer demand and posed a genuine deflationary risk. Deflation discourages spending (consumers defer purchases expecting lower prices) and can create a self-reinforcing downward spiral.

**The 2021–2022 Inflation Surge:** The jump from 1.23% (2020) to 4.70% (2021) and then to 8.00% (2022) represents a 53× increase in inflation over three years. This was the most severe inflationary episode of the decade, driven by simultaneous demand stimulus, supply chain bottlenecks, and energy price shocks.

**The Soft Landing (2023–2025):** The return from 8% to 2.6% without triggering a recession (GDP continued growing at 2.5% in 2025) represents a textbook "soft landing" scenario — tightening monetary conditions controlled inflation while economic activity was preserved.

**Outlook:** At 2.6%, inflation is 0.3 percentage points below 2024 (2.9%) and now within the typical central bank target range of 2–3%. Further disinflation to the 2% target is likely if wage growth remains moderate.

## 6. Key Risks & Recommendations

### Near-Term Risks

1. **Inflation Re-acceleration:** If inflation does not continue its descent toward 2%, interest rates may need to remain restrictive, threatening the 2.5% growth trajectory. The 2021–2022 episode shows how quickly inflation can escape containment.

2. **Unemployment Creep:** The 2023–2024 uptick from 3.61% to 4.05% warrants monitoring. If higher interest rates continue slowing hiring, unemployment could test the 4.5% level — a threshold that historically precedes more pronounced labor market deterioration.

3. **Capital Depreciation Acceleration:** With $2.27T in annual depreciation in 2025 (vs. $1.36T in 2015), infrastructure and capital maintenance costs are mounting. Insufficient reinvestment could gradually erode productive capacity.

4. **GNP–GDP Divergence:** While the widening spread is currently positive, a reversal (where foreign earnings at home begin to exceed domestic earnings abroad) would indicate weakening external investment positioning.

### Strategic Recommendations

1. **Sustain the Soft Landing:** Monetary policy should continue gradual easing as inflation cools. The target of 2.0% inflation is within reach given the current trajectory. Premature easing risks re-igniting inflation; over-tightening risks the labor market.

2. **Support Capital Investment:** Given rising depreciation costs, policies that incentivize domestic capital investment (tax credits, infrastructure spending) will help replace aging infrastructure and sustain productive capacity.

3. **Diversify Sector Exposure:** The 60% services concentration should prompt investment in resilient, countercyclical industries. Strengthening manufacturing and high-value-added industry (from 30% to 32–33%) would provide a buffer against service-sector shocks.

4. **Labor Force Participation:** With unemployment near historic lows (3.9%), future growth will increasingly depend on productivity gains and labor force participation expansion rather than absorption of excess unemployment.

5. **Monitor the GNP–GDP Spread:** The growing positive spread should be tracked annually. Policies supporting domestic firms' foreign investment and protecting existing overseas income streams will sustain this advantage.
